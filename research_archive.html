Archived projects

MHD shear flow instabilities
primary collaborators: David Hughes, Stephen Griffiths (Leeds), Eyal Heifetz (Tel Aviv)
papers: MGH17 PRF, MGH16 JFM, HNMU15 JFM

As part of my PhD project with some spillover on to my first post-doc I was primarily interested in the instability characteristics of shear flow instabilities in the magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) system, motivated by solar dynamics and the tachocline (in analogy with the ocean thermocline). I investigated extensively the linear onset of shear instability (mostly of the Kelvin-Helmholtz type but in the horizontal) and the nonlinear equilibration of the instability.

I analysed extensively using mathematical analyses, idealised profiles and asymptotic analyses for the linear instability problem in a shallow water type system in MHD. The main achievements are: rigourous semi-circle theorems for the eigenvalues associated with unstable modes; deriving and extending the long-wave asymptotic framework and confirming it with numerical calculations; propose the mechanistic interpretation for MHD shear instability arising from interactions of modified vorticity waves (cf. Rossby waves in rotating stratified fluid dynamics).

Double semi-circle theorem in the SWMHD system (cf. Hughes & Tobias, 2001)

One aspect that I extended during my post-doc (though the resulting paper actually arrived before my first PhD paper) was on the mechanistic interpretation leading to MHD shear instability. Based on the Counter-propagating Rossby Wave mechanism, we worked out how the magnetic field modifies the interaction arising vorticity anomalies and provide a unifying explanation for why unstable flows can be stabilised by a magnetic field, and why some stable flows are destablised by the magnetic field.

Schematic of CRW mechanism: two outer vorticity waves sandwiching a stabilising standing wave

I investigated the nonlinear evolution of MHD unstable flows. What is well-known is that at large enough field strengths (but weak enough such that the linear instability is only marginally modified, and the initial energy ratios are small) the vortex roll up is disrupted by MHD feedbacks, something we call vortex disruption. Our contribution here is to provide a scaling argument based on some old work in flux expulsion with a linear dependence on the magnetic Reynolds number. The scaling was confirmed using a pseudo-spectral code I wrote from scratch, which differs from some previous works where the magnetic Reynolds numbers is not entirely well-defined because of the use of grid-scale diffusivities. Here we provide an additional argument the definition of "weak" is ambiguous, as a large-scale field that is "weak" can be "strong" at small-scales (which is the case here), which can effect on the large-scale dynamics.

Evolution of shear layer flow at different background magnetic fields.

Non-Boussinesq shear flow instabilities
primary collaborators: Eyal Heifetz (Tel Aviv)
papers: HM15 PoF

This was a short project which is not dissimilar to the MHD CRW mechanism above. Again we elucidate how stratification modifies the CRW mechanism (effectively through baroclinic torque). The additional physics we elucidate is the symmetry breaking aspect arising from not making the Boussinesq approximation, arising from differences in pressure anomalies.

Schematic of torque generation and asymmetry in the resulting pressure anomalies

Geostrophic MHD turbulence
primary collaborators: Pat Diamond (UCSD)
papers: none although there is a proceedings entry somewhere...

This was a short project that I carried out whilst I was a first year PhD student at the (formerly) ISIMA summer school at UCSC for six weeks. Nothing major came from it (except a double semi-circle theorem and some work in relation to the derivation of the magnetic quasi-geostrophic equations based on work by Peter Gilman but it got me very interested in rotating turbulence and set the ground work for a lot of my current research to do with rotating stratified fluid dynamics. I might get back to this given Peter Gilman has recently revisited magneto-baroclinic dynamics, maybe.
