Research overview

*** Please feel free to get in touch (see ABOUT for e-mail) if you have questions, interested in collaborating or want to work with me as a post-doc/postgrad/undergrad! ***

I am generally interested in fluid dynamical problems in rotating stratified systems, motivated by geophysical and astrophysical systems (mostly focusing on the ocean). I employ a variety of computational and mathematical tools to tackle related problems. Areas I have worked on and/or are particularly interested in are:

  • Wave/eddy-mean flow interactions (global ocean circulation, eddy parameterisation)
  • General Circulation Model developments
  • Inverse methods for oceanography
  • Fluid instabilities (in particular shear instabilities)
  • Tracer mixing/stirring

Numerical models play a very large role in my research though I am relatively agnostic about which models to use. I go from bespoke small-scale models (largely in MATLAB and Python) to global circulation models (NEMO and MITgcm). Recent projects (archived projects)

GEMOETRIC (Geometry and Energetics of Ocean Mesoscale Eddies and Their Rectified Impact on Climate)
primary collaborators: David Marshall (Oxford), James Maddison (Edinburgh), Scott Bachman (NCAR)
papers: MMMM18 JPO, MMMB17 OM, BMMM17 OM

(David has a concise summary of it on his webpage.) In its present form GEOMETRIC is, from a practical point of view, a reworking of the well-known Gent-McWilliams parameterisation for mesoscale eddies arising from baroclinic instability. How the baroclinic eddies are represented in a numerical model can lead to large changes in the global ocean circulation through changes to the Southern Ocean residual overturning circulation.

Spilhaus projection of the ocean
schematic of SO RMOC taken from Burke et al. (2015), GRL

I have implemented the GEOMETRIC scheme in various forms into a hierarchy of numerical models, ranging from custom models of the Southern Ocean to global ocean circulation models in NEMO. In particular, we demonstrate the use of GEOMETRIC leads to improvements of global circulation characteristics to changed in surface wind forcing, notably the eddy saturation and the eddy compensation phenomenon. This in turn have implications for things like energy, carbon and oxygen cycles for changes to tracer ventilation.

Gauge-invariant diagnoses of eddy diffusivity through inverse methods
primary collaborators: James Maddison (Edinburgh), David Marshall (Oxford)
papers: MMM16 OM

Part of the problem in eddy parameterisation is on how one specifies the eddy diffusivities or the eddy transport coefficients. As a way to constrain the theory one way is to employ high resolution numerical simulation as model truths and diagnose the coefficients accordingly. However there is the technical problem of gauge freedom, where the eddy forcing is invariant up to a rotational term, but the diagnoses of the coefficients cares about this rotational term. As a way round this we employ a definition of the eddy force function, which restores the uniqueness property in the associated diagnoses when boundaries are involved. Additionally, we employ an inverse method approach (cf. 3DVar in variational data assimilation) which also bypasses problems to do with having noisy data causing numerical problems.

Diagnoses of the GM coefficient in a three-layer model

There are some suggestions of extending this to related plasma turbulence problems (in collaboration with Yusuke Kosuga at Kyushu), watch this space maybe.

Nonlinear equilibration of baroclinic lifecycles
primary collaborators: Nili Harnik, Eyal Heifetz (Tel Aviv), David Dritschel (St. Andrews)
papers: none yet...

Baroclinic instability is ubiquitous in rotating stratified systems (e.g. giant gas planets, Earth's atmosphere and ocean) and its associated wave/eddy-mean interaction has consequences for the modelling of sub-grid physics (e.g. GEOMETRIC project above for ocean climate). While we understand the instability characteristics fairly well, to my mind there are a few details to do with the nonlinear development and equilibration that can be sharpened. We are investigating various aspects of baroclinic lifecycles in a simple two-layer quasi-geostrophic system using the CASL/CLAM code developed by David Dritschel.
